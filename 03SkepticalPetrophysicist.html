Gamma Ray Index vs Shale Volume Transforms





You know about the Arps Approximation. If you do formation evaluation you probably can write it down without looking for a reference. Do you know where it comes from? Do you know where it comes from? Have you ever read Arps' paper?

I fooled around with this data for a long time trying to develop a better salinity-temperature- resistivity transform, looking to capture the curvature of the iso-salinity (or iso-concentration) lines which are suggested by the data. The "approximation" in Arps' approximation is to replace the curved iso-saliniity lines with straight lines. In manipulating the data graphically in search of trends, I noticed that the change in conductivity with respect to temperature is proportional to temperature and a function of salinity. This last sentence is easily written down as an elementary differential equation. The solution is a definite integral between a referece temperature and a formation temperature. If two different formation tempertures are chosen, and the resulting equations are "ratioed", the funtion of salinity cancels out, and the resulting formula is the Arps approximation. The whole derivation from differential equation to final formula following in about five - very simple to follow - lines of math. It takes much more ink to explain that it (as above) than to do it. Of course, to agree with me that a differential equation is simple, you'd have to know what a differential equation is in the first place. Still, if you'd had calculus and stopped short of differential equations (or you've forgot the techniques learned long ago) you can still appreciate the algebra, once the differential equation is integrated; that is, solved. The result is still the Arps approximation; i.e., it is not any more accurate merely because it has be derived analytically, as opposed to Arps' statistical approch. But it may give you comfort, as it does me, that it is really very easy to explain.

Deriving Arps' Approximation from a Differential Equation





Have you ever seen a resistivity log "flatline" at about 2000 ohm-meters?

2000 is a "big" number, the highest on the resistivity logarithmic scale. Does this mean that there is "too much resistivity to measure"? Well, that is not the most accurate way to think about what you're looking at. In fact, the physical situration is just about the exact opposite. It might sould like quibbling about semantics, but it isn't. The actual situration is that there is too little conductivity for the insturment to respond to accurately. The formation conductivity is too close to zero for the instrument to have any output except electronic noise. This happens when the conductivity is less than about 0.0005 Siemens per meter; the reciprocal of the apparent conductivity would then be 2000 ohm-meters and the service company, not wanting you to interpret electronic noise as something in the formation, truncates (or clips) the apparent resistivity curve at 2000 (sometimes less depending on the instrument) ohm-meters.

Do Induction Logs "Saturate" at High Resistivity?





You may not know that in spite of several claims to the contrary, the Archie model had eluded attempts to derive it from more fundatmental first principles. It appears that the thought never occured to anyone to try. Well, no one was doing "theoretical petrophysics". Still, you would have thought . . . Parita Sen, then at the Schlumberger-Doll Lab published a claim of deriving Archie's law from a first principle in 1982, the the "first principle" was in fact the Hani-Bruggeman "effective medium" model, hardly a first principle, and the porosity exponent thus derived was 3/2 exactly rather than the Archie approximate 4/2. So, it's the 1980s and no one is succeeding.

That is ... until the early 1990s when Dr. David Herrick, having worked long and hard to understand the resistivity interpretation paradigm in use up to that time, decided it simply could not be understood on the basis of Ohm's law and arm-waving. He finished his thinking while a researcher at Mobil's Dallas Research Laboratory and published his findings in 1993 and 1994 with his coauthor, me.

The basis of Herrick's theory was to state a set of three physically obvious and eminently reasonable "first principles". These are: The bulk conductivity of a class of rocks of interest is proportional to: (1) the conductivity of the brine in the rock; (2) the amount of brine in the rock; (3) the geometrical configuration (i.e., brine geometry) of the brine in the rock. The restriction of "a class of rocks" refers to rocks whose conductivity depends upon the brine (and only the brine) in the rock. There are other restrictions such as the rocks should have primary porosity only and no fractures. The class of rocks corresponds to rocks where Archie's model works well and are therefore called "Archie Rocks". The new model was first dubbed the "Electrical Efficiency Theory" and later the name shifted to the "Geometrical Factor Theory". The Archie model is a special case of the GFT.

I always had a nagging suspicion that the model had not been fully developed, and introduced percolation threshold concepts into a similar model (PPTT) in 2007. The Archie model was also a special case of this theory; however, the GFT and PPTT models were not the same; I suspected one of them must therefore be in error. Finally, by fully generalizing the GFT, I was able to show that the original GFT and PPTT are both special cases of a more general model, a generalized geometrical factor model (GGFT). It turns out that GGFT is able to accomodate the "classical" shaly-sand models (Waxman-Smits & Dual Water) as special cases.

However, one thing that remained constant throughout the development is Herrick's three first principles. If you absorb this video, I guarantee your insight into how rocks conduct electricity, and understanding of the various saturation models, is going to improve over what it was when you got your popcorn and sat down to watch.

Generalizing the Geometrical Factor Theory





In 1968 Waxman and Smits, representing Shell, and benefiting from Shell's reputation as the industy's leading experts in petrophysics, trading upon G. E. Archie's pioneering discoveries, introduced a model for the conductivity interpretation of oil-bearing shaly sands. The model relied upon electrochemical theory; however, the paper was not tutorial in any way. In addition to its being non-tutorial and written mainly to their fellow electrochemists at Shell, is among the most inscrutably written papers in the oil-patch literature.

The Waxman-Smits model acquired the patina of being derived from first principles mainly from its invincible impenetrableness and formation evaluator's credulousness due to Shell's reputation in petrophysics. The the model was challenged a decade later by Clavier, Coates, and Dumanoir of Schlumberger and their alternative Dual Water Model. However, Shell being one of Schlumberger's major custormers, the challenge was delivered with a velvet touch. Moreover, the Dual Water Model had problems of its own.

This video deconstucts the theoretical basis of the Waxman-Smits model on its own terms. I show that it is not, as it claims to be, a parallel conductivity model. This is not to say that it is not a "useful" model. It is certainly better than "no model", and its parameters are measurable in the laboratory. It has been an industry standard model for a long time. I hope this video clarifies some of its foundations.
